Phase 1 — Receive & Unbox
Physical

Patient Receives Box

Package arrives with the light therapy device, iPad (pre-loaded with app), and printed instructions.

Design note: Include printed quick-start card in the box. Addresses fear-of-technology barrier for older/less tech-savvy patients. The card should mirror the onboarding flow — same language, same steps — so nothing feels unfamiliar.
Hooked — External Trigger: The physical box is the first trigger. Make unboxing feel like opening something special, not medical equipment. The printed card is a fallback trigger if the screen feels intimidating.
Physical

Open Box & Find Materials

Patient opens the box, finds the device, iPad, cable, and printed quick-start card. Card says: "Start here."

UX: The printed card should have 3–4 steps max. Large type. Illustrations, not photos. Arrow pointing to "Turn on iPad" as the single next action.
Phase 2 — Onboarding
Patient turns on iPad → App launches
Screen

Welcome Screen

Warm, calm welcome. One sentence about what this is. Single button: "Let's get started."

Hooked — Investment phase begins: Every tap the patient makes during setup is a micro-investment. The more they put in now, the more committed they become to completing the trial. Keep each step tiny so the investment accumulates without friction.
Screen

Onboarding: What to Expect

3–4 glanceable cards (swipe or auto-advance) that cover:

① This is a daily morning routine — same time, every day
② Each session is ~30 min of light while you watch something you enjoy
③ Good sleep makes treatment work better — no naps, stick to your window
④ We'll guide you every step of the way
Key principle: Onboarding mirrors the printed instructions — same content, less reading. Designed to be understood at a glance. No walls of text.
UX: Each card = one illustration + one sentence. Progress dots at bottom. Patient should spend ~15 seconds per card max.
SuperBetter — Epic Meaning: Frame this as a quest the patient is choosing to undertake. They aren't "assigned treatment" — they're starting something that could meaningfully improve how they feel. Give the daily routine a sense of purpose, not obligation.

System

Calculate Recommended Window

System processes baseline data to determine optimal light therapy window and wake time recommendation.

UX: Brief loading moment — "Building your schedule..." — gives weight to the personalization. Don't make it instant; a 2–3 second pause signals the system is doing real work with their answers.
Phase 4 — Set Tomorrow's Alarm
Screen

Your Recommended Window

Show the patient their personalized light therapy window for tomorrow. Clear, large time display. Explain briefly why this window matters.

SuperBetter — Power-up: The recommended window is the patient's first "power-up." It's personal, it's based on their data, and it gives them a concrete action for tomorrow. This is the moment the trial stops being abstract and becomes real.
User Action

Set Alarm for Tomorrow

Patient confirms or adjusts the suggested wake-up time. Single tap to set alarm. This is the commitment moment.

Critical: This is the key action of Day 0. Everything before builds to this. Everything after is bonus. The alarm is what bridges today's setup to tomorrow's first session.
Hooked — Investment: Setting the alarm is the patient's biggest investment of Day 0. They're making a commitment to future action. This stored value increases the likelihood they follow through tomorrow.

If patient skips entertainment setup, schedule a push notification for later that evening: "Quick tip: log into your favorite app before bed so it's ready for tomorrow morning."

Phase 6 — Dashboard Empty State
Screen

Dashboard — Empty State

Patient lands on the main dashboard for the first time. Empty but not bare — it previews what's coming.

Score area → placeholder: "Your score will appear after your first session"
Session tracker → Day 1 highlighted, rest empty: "Your streak starts tomorrow"
Tomorrow's session card → Time + "Your first session is set for [time]"
Progress area → gentle preview of what gets filled over time
Key principle: The empty state should make the patient feel oriented, not overwhelmed. They should think "I know exactly what happens next" — not "there's so much to fill in."
UX: The dashboard should feel like a calm room they've just walked into, with labeled spots for things that will arrive. Think hotel room with your name on the door, not an empty spreadsheet.
SuperBetter — Quest preview: The empty dashboard is the quest map before the journey begins. The patient can see the path laid out. Seeing the empty tracker with Day 1 highlighted creates anticipation — a desire to "start filling it in." This is the variable reward hook: what will my score be? What will my streak look like?
Day 0 Complete
Complete

First Day Done

Patient has completed setup. Alarm is set. They know what tomorrow looks like. The app is quiet until morning.

Hooked — Trigger loaded: The alarm is the internal trigger for tomorrow. The entertainment apps remove friction from the action. The personalized schedule is the variable reward waiting. The questionnaire answers and alarm setting are the investment. The full Hook cycle is primed and ready for Day 1.

Edge Cases & Considerations

What if the patient is too tired to finish setup? — Save progress. Let them resume. Show a clear "pick up where you left off" screen. The questionnaire should auto-save after each section.

What if they get the device in the evening? — Fine. The alarm still gets set for tomorrow. Remind them to charge the iPad overnight.

What if they skip the questionnaire entirely? — Don't let them. This data is required for the clinical trial. But break it into small enough pieces that it never feels heavy.

What if they struggle with the technology? — The printed card is the safety net. It should be good enough to get them from "box open" to "alarm set" without the iPad confusing them.
